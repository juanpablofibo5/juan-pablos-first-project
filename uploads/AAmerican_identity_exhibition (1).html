What Does It Mean
to Be American?

Central Thesis

To be American means living with a contradiction: a country that promises freedom, equality, and opportunity for everyone, but has never fully delivered on that promise. American identity is not defined by being perfect — it is defined by the constant struggle to get closer to those ideals, through ambition, patriotism, resilience, and the ongoing belief that things can be better.

Artifact A · Film
Top Gun
Directed by Tony Scott · 1986 · Paramount Pictures
Characteristics of American Identity
1. Pride & Patriotism
Maverick wears his U.S. Navy uniform with confidence and pride. The film shows that being a military pilot is more than just a job — it is a way of representing your country and showing how much you love it.
2. Strength & Power
The American military is shown as the best in the world — fast, powerful, and always in control. The film sends a clear message: the U.S. is strong, and nobody should mess with it.
3. Competition
The pilots compete hard to be number one at Top Gun school. This reflects a key American belief: if you work hard and push yourself, you can rise to the top. The best person wins — not luck, but effort.
4. Individualism
Maverick breaks the rules and does things his own way. In the end, his individual bravery saves the day. This is a classic American idea — one person, through their own courage, can make the difference.

Left: The Space Race — real-life American ambition and patriotism in action. Right: The American city skyline — symbol of power, opportunity, and national pride.

Connection to Thesis
Top Gun shows one of the biggest tensions in American identity: the idea that you should stand out as an individual, but also serve something bigger than yourself. Maverick wants to be free and do things his way — but at the end of the day, he uses that freedom to fight for his country. This back-and-forth between "I" and "we," between personal freedom and national duty, is something Americans have always struggled with. Top Gun does not resolve that tension — it celebrates both sides of it at the same time, which is exactly what American identity does.
Historical Evidence
1960s
The Space Race is a perfect real-life version of Top Gun's spirit. The U.S. and the Soviet Union were racing to see who could get to the moon first — and in 1969, America won. It was not just about science; it was about national pride and proving that the U.S. was the strongest country in the world.
1970s–80s
During the Cold War, the U.S. and the Soviet Union were in a constant competition over who had the better military. President Reagan's Strategic Defense Initiative (1983) — known as "Star Wars" — was a plan to build a missile defense system in space. Like Top Gun, it was all about showing that America's technology and strength could not be beaten.
Modern
After the attacks on September 11, 2001, the U.S. launched military operations in Afghanistan. Thousands of young Americans signed up to join the military out of a sense of duty and pride — the same feeling that drives Maverick throughout the film.
Artifact B · Song
"9 to 5"
Dolly Parton · 1980 · RCA Records
Characteristics of American Identity
1. Hard Work
The workers in the song show up every single day, put in long hours, and give everything they have. This is one of the most basic American values — the belief that working hard is honorable and is how you build a better life.
2. Inequality
The women in the song do all the hard work, but their boss gets the credit and the money. This shows that America does not always treat people fairly — especially women and working-class people — even when they follow all the rules.
3. Ambition
The workers want more from life — better pay, respect, and a real chance to move up. That desire to improve your situation is one of the most American things there is.
4. Frustration & Resilience
Instead of just accepting the unfair treatment, the workers push back. Standing up against injustice — through protest and collective action — is a long tradition in American history.

Left: Workers' protests — a long American tradition of fighting for fair treatment. Right: The March on Washington (1963) — when Americans demanded equality together.

Connection to Thesis
"9 to 5" shows the gap between the American Dream and real life. The workers believe in the Dream — they are willing to work hard, they have ambition, they want to succeed. But the system they are part of does not give them a fair chance. This is the same contradiction the thesis talks about: America promises that hard work leads to success, but for many people that promise has not been kept. And yet the song does not give up. It fights back. That resilience is also deeply American.
Historical Evidence
Progressive Era
In the early 1900s, workers — especially young immigrant women — had extremely difficult jobs in factories. The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire of 1911 killed 146 workers because the building had no proper safety exits. This tragedy showed how badly some employers treated their workers, and it pushed the government to create better labor laws.
1950s–60s
In the 1960s, more women were entering the workforce than ever before, but they were still paid less than men for doing the same job. The Equal Pay Act of 1963 was passed to start fixing this, but the problem did not go away overnight.
Modern
Today, the fight Parton sang about in 1980 is still going on. The gender pay gap still exists, and the #MeToo movement (2017) brought the world's attention to how women are still mistreated in workplaces. The song speaks to a problem that has never fully been solved in America.
Artifact C · Iconic Architecture
The Hollywood Sign
Hollywood Hills, Los Angeles · Established 1923
Characteristics of American Identity
1. Ambition
For over a century, people have packed their bags and moved to Hollywood with one goal: to make it big. The sign represents that drive — the belief that if you want it badly enough and work for it, you can become somebody.
2. Fame & Recognition
Hollywood is the home of stars. America has a unique obsession with fame — with being known, seen, and admired. The sign sits above the city like a reminder that becoming famous is a goal worth chasing.
3. The American Dream
The Hollywood Sign is the American Dream made visible. It says: anyone can come here, work hard, and change their life completely. You do not need a famous family or money — just talent and the will to try.
4. Opportunity
Hollywood has always attracted people from all over the world who believe America gives them a real shot at a better life. The sign stands for the idea that the door is open — you just have to walk through it.

Left: The American city — built by ambition and opportunity. Right: America reaching for the stars — the Dream applied to exploration itself.

Connection to Thesis
The Hollywood Sign is one of the most powerful symbols of the American Dream — but it also shows the Dream's dark side. For every person who made it to the big screen, thousands of others arrived with the same hope and went home with nothing. The sign shines bright, but its light does not reach everyone equally. This is exactly the contradiction at the heart of American identity: the country promises opportunity for anyone willing to reach for it, but the reality is that not everyone gets the same chance.
Historical Evidence
Interwar
In the 1920s and 1930s, Hollywood grew into the entertainment capital of the world. Stars like Charlie Chaplin and Bette Davis became household names — ordinary people who became legends. This was the American Dream in action, right at a time when the Great Depression was making life incredibly hard for most Americans.
1950s
In the 1950s, Hollywood films showed the perfect American life. But the Hollywood Blacklist was destroying careers behind the scenes. Anyone suspected of communist beliefs could be fired and banned — showing that the Dream had limits for those who said the wrong things.
Modern
Today, Hollywood is a powerful tool of American soft power. But movements like #OscarsSoWhite (2015) exposed that for decades, Black actors, Latino actors, and women had far fewer chances to succeed, even when equally talented.
Artifact D · Non-Fiction Text
"A More Perfect Union"
Barack Obama · March 18, 2008 · Philadelphia, Pennsylvania
Characteristics of American Identity
1. Equality-Focused
Obama argues that every person in America deserves to be treated fairly, no matter their skin color or background. He calls on the country to live up to the promise it made in the Declaration of Independence — that all people are equal.
2. Contradictory
Obama points out the central contradiction of America: the country was founded on the idea that all people are equal, but it was also built on slavery. He argues this is not ancient history — it still affects life in America today.
3. Diverse
Obama himself is a symbol of America's diversity — his father was Kenyan, his mother was American. He uses his own story to argue that America's strength comes from being made up of so many different people.
4. Hopeful
Even after talking about painful history and racism, Obama insists that things can get better. Hope is not naive here — it is a choice to keep trying, and it is a deeply American instinct.

Left: The March on Washington, 1963 — the movement Obama's speech honors and continues. Right: The Declaration of Independence — the founding promise he says America must still live up to.

Connection to Thesis
Obama's speech is basically the thesis out loud. He says directly that America is a country built on a contradiction: it promised freedom and equality, but for much of its history those promises only applied to some people. He uses the phrase "a more perfect union" — from the Constitution — to say that America was never meant to be finished or perfect. It was meant to keep improving. Being American, in Obama's view, means accepting that the country has problems and choosing to fix them anyway. That connects all five artifacts: they all show Americans still trying to close the gap between the promise and the reality.
Historical Evidence
Rev. America
In 1776, the Declaration of Independence declared that "all men are created equal." But the man who wrote those words, Thomas Jefferson, owned over 600 enslaved people. This is the contradiction Obama talks about — America's most important founding document promised equality while the country was built on slavery.
Antebellum
In the 1840s and 1850s, abolitionists like Frederick Douglass and Harriet Tubman fought hard to end slavery. Douglass gave a powerful speech in 1852 asking what the Fourth of July meant to an enslaved person — basically making the same point Obama would make 156 years later.
Jim Crow Era
After the Civil War, Black Americans were given rights through the 14th and 15th Amendments — but then Jim Crow laws took those rights away. Schools were segregated, Black voters were blocked from voting, and racial violence went unpunished. Obama's speech directly responds to this history.
1950s–60s
The Civil Rights Movement pushed hard for real change. The March on Washington in 1963, the Civil Rights Act of 1964, and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 were all huge steps forward. Obama's entire political career would have been impossible without the people who marched and sacrificed during this era.
Artifact E · Political Advertisement
"America First" (2016)
Donald Trump Presidential Campaign · 2016
Characteristics of American Identity
1. Nationalism
The message is simple: America should put its own people first, before helping other countries. Jobs, borders, and security for Americans — that is the priority. Everything else comes second.
2. Protective
The ad frames America as a country under threat — from people coming in illegally, from foreign countries taking American jobs, and from weak leaders. Protecting America is presented as the highest form of patriotism.
3. Division
The language creates a clear split between "real" Americans and everyone else — immigrants, elites, foreigners. This "us vs. them" way of thinking has a long history in America, and it divides people instead of bringing them together.
4. Powerful
The ad says America used to be great and powerful, but weak leaders let it get worse. The solution is strength — a tough president who will make America feared and respected again.

Left: The Trail of Tears (1830) — an early example of deciding who "belongs" in America and removing those who don't. Right: McCarthy Hearings (1950s) — dividing Americans into the loyal and the "threats."

Connection to Thesis
"America First" shows a side of American identity that is just as real as Obama's vision — even though it is very different. While Obama opens America up by saying it belongs to everyone who shares its ideals, Trump's message draws a border: America belongs to certain people first, and others are a threat to it. This idea has come up over and over in American history. Both visions are genuinely American. The fact that they exist at the same time, pulling the country in opposite directions, is itself the contradiction that the thesis is about.
Historical Evidence
Jacksonian
President Andrew Jackson signed the Indian Removal Act in 1830, which forced Native American tribes off their lands. Thousands died on the journey known as the Trail of Tears. This was an early and violent example of deciding who "belongs" in America and forcing everyone else out.
Interwar
In 1940 and 1941, the original America First Committee campaigned to keep the U.S. out of World War II, arguing the country should focus on itself. Trump's use of the same slogan in 2016 was a direct connection to that same "protect our own" tradition.
1950s–60s
McCarthyism (1950–54) was a period when U.S. Senator Joseph McCarthy accused hundreds of Americans of being secret communists. People lost their jobs and were publicly shamed without real proof. It created the same "us vs. them" fear that "America First" uses.
Modern
Since the 1990s, debates about immigration, border security, and jobs going overseas have been some of the biggest political fights in America. "America First" taps directly into those fears — the latest version of a very old American argument about who the country is really for.
Comparative Analysis

Same Country, Different Visions

Looking at all five artifacts together, two big tensions keep coming up: the gap between the promise of opportunity and the reality of inequality, and the question of whether America is a country that belongs to everyone or only to some. These tensions are not accidents — they are what American identity is made of.

Comparison 1 — Who Is America For?
"A More Perfect Union"
Barack Obama, 2008
View of America: A work in progress that can always get better
Who belongs: Everyone who shares the ideal of equality
Tone: Hopeful and honest about history
Message: Unite across differences to build something better
"America First"
Donald Trump, 2016
View of America: A great country being weakened by the wrong people
Who belongs: "Real" Americans who need to be protected first
Tone: Urgent and protective
Message: Put America's own people ahead of the rest of the world
Analysis: Two Visions of the Same Country

Obama and Trump have more in common than it might seem at first. Both say they love America. Both say the country has serious problems. And both are trying to convince Americans to follow their vision. So what makes them so different?

The real difference is in how they answer: "Who is America for?" Obama's answer is: everyone. He argues that America has not lived up to its founding promise — but it can, if people work together. Diversity is not a problem; it is one of America's greatest strengths.

Trump's answer is more narrow. His message says America should put its own citizens first — especially those who feel left behind. Where Obama says "let's come together," Trump says "let's protect what's ours." This is not a brand-new idea — Americans have been having this exact argument since the country was founded.

Together, these two artifacts show the oldest question in American history: who does this country really belong to? Neither side has won that argument — and that ongoing fight is itself part of what it means to be American.

Comparison 2 — Does Hard Work Pay Off?
Top Gun
Film, 1986
Hard work leads to: Recognition, promotion, and success
The system is: Fair — the best person wins
The Dream: Very real and within reach
Who gets ahead: The brave individual who pushes themselves
"9 to 5"
Dolly Parton, 1980
Hard work leads to: Being taken advantage of and ignored
The system is: Rigged — the boss always wins
The Dream: Promised, but out of reach for many workers
Who gets ahead: Those already in power
Analysis: Does Hard Work Actually Pay Off?

Both Top Gun and "9 to 5" are about people who work incredibly hard and believe that effort should be rewarded. Maverick trains every day and risks his life. The office workers show up every single day and give everything to their jobs. Both groups believe in the American idea that if you put in the effort, you will get something back.

But the results could not be more different. Maverick breaks the rules, takes risks, and still ends up being celebrated as a hero. The system rewards him. For the workers in "9 to 5," the opposite happens — their boss takes credit for their ideas, pays them less, and faces no consequences at all. They did everything right, and it still was not enough.

Put these two artifacts side by side and the contradiction becomes crystal clear: America genuinely believes that hard work leads to success — but that is only true for some people. For women, working-class people, and many minorities, the system has not always played fair.

Top Gun shows the American Dream working perfectly. "9 to 5" shows it failing. Together, they prove that both things are true at once — and that is the contradiction at the heart of what it means to be American.

Bringing It All Together

All five artifacts — a film, a song, a landmark, a speech, and a political ad — tell versions of the same story. America has always had big, powerful ideals: freedom, equality, opportunity for all. And America has always struggled — sometimes failed badly — to actually live up to those ideals.

Top Gun shows the pride and strength. "9 to 5" shows the frustration and inequality. The Hollywood Sign shows the Dream and who gets left out of it. Obama's speech names the contradiction honestly and calls the country to do better. Trump's ad shows what happens when people decide the answer is to protect their own, not to include everyone.

To be American is not to be one thing. It is to be all of these things at once — the hope and the failure, the pride and the protest, the promise and the gap that still exists between that promise and real life. American identity is not finished. It is still being argued over, fought for, and shaped — right now, today.
